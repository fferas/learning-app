## Critical Caveat: IB PYP Does Not Prescribe a Fixed Content Checklist

The most important architectural decision for any IB-first app is understanding what the IB PYP *does and does not* mandate. Per the [IB official curriculum overview](https://www.ibo.org/programmes/primary-years-programme/curriculum/the-learner/):

> "The PYP curriculum framework begins with the premise that students are agents of their own learning and partners in the learning process."

**What this means practically:**

1. **No grade-level content standards.** The PYP publishes *phase-based* continuums (Phases 1–4 in the 2025 subject guides), not grade-by-grade checklists. The [2025 updated subject guides](https://www.toddleapp.com/learn/blog-post/2025-pyp-updates-your-guide-to-whats-changing-2/) (released April 2025) explicitly state phases are "not age- or grade-bound, but instead represent increasingly complex ways of thinking and understanding."

2. **Each school writes its own Programme of Inquiry (POI).** Science content is almost entirely context-driven by the school's six transdisciplinary units. An app cannot replicate what a specific Dubai IB school will cover.

3. **Mathematics and Language have published scope and sequence continuums** (pre-2025: the 2009 maths scope and sequence; the language scope and sequence) that are the closest thing to learning objectives. Post-April 2025, IB released new subject guides replacing these documents. The pre-2025 continuums remain the best publicly accessible granular reference.

4. **Early years (ages 3–6) are a protected developmental zone.** The [IB Early Years page](https://www.ibo.org/primary-years-programme-in-the-early-years/) and the [Early Learner document](https://www.copenhageninternational.school/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/The-Early-Learner-IB-Primary-Years-Programme.pdf) emphasize play-as-inquiry and explicitly discourage worksheets and predetermined skill drills for this age band.

5. **App positioning:** Frame all modules as *supporting and extending* PYP inquiry rather than replacing or mimicking school instruction. Gamified daily practice is most defensible as home-practice reinforcement of conceptual understanding.

### 2A. KG1 — Mathematics (Phase 1)

Sources: [IBO Maths Scope and Sequence via IC Beirut](https://www.ic.edu.lb/uploaded/programs/IB_PYP_Program/PYP_math_scope_and_sequence.pdf); [ISoP PYP Maths Scope and Sequence via Scribd](https://www.scribd.com/document/685049078/ISoP-PYP-Mathematics-Scope-and-Sequence)

#### Number

**Conceptual understandings:**
- Numbers are a naming system
- Numbers can be used in many ways for different purposes
- Numbers are connected to each other through relationships
- Making connections between experiences with number develops number sense

**Knowledge and skills:**
| Category | Specific skills |
|----------|----------------|
| Construct meaning | Understand one-to-one correspondence; understand that the last number counted describes the quantity (cardinality principle); understand conservation of number; understand that numbers can be combined and partitioned; understand relative magnitude (more/less); recognize groups of 0–5 without counting (subitizing); understand whole-part relationships |
| Transfer to symbols | Connect number names and numerals to quantities they represent |
| Apply | Count to determine number of objects in a set; use number words and numerals in real-life situations; use language of comparison (more, less, first, second); subitize in real-life situations |

**ISoP KG1-specific outcomes:** Sort and classify objects by colour, shape, size and type; count and match sets of objects to numerals 1–10; compare numbers 1–10 using the language of more/fewer and less/greater; read and write numbers 1–10; order numbers 1–10 on a number line; identify missing numbers in a sequence 1–10

#### Pattern and Function

**Conceptual understandings:**
- Patterns and sequences occur in everyday situations
- Patterns repeat and grow

**Skills:**
- Understand patterns in everyday situations (sounds, actions, objects, nature)
- Describe patterns using words, drawings, symbols, materials, actions, numbers
- Extend and create patterns

**ISoP KG1-specific outcomes:** Recognise, describe, extend and create patterns using real objects and drawings

#### Measurement

**Conceptual understandings:**
- Measurement involves comparing objects and events
- Objects have attributes measured using non-standard units
- Events can be ordered and sequenced

**Skills:**
- Understand and describe attributes of real objects (longer, shorter, heavier, full, empty, hotter, colder)
- Understand events can be sequenced (before, after, bedtime, storytime, today, tomorrow)
- Compare length, mass, capacity using non-standard units
- Describe observations in real-life situations
- Use non-standard units to solve measurement problems

**ISoP KG1-specific outcomes:** Introduce language of length (long/short), mass (heavy/light), capacity (full/empty, more/less); identify, compare and describe attributes of real objects; put sets of real objects in order of size and length; identify, describe and sequence events in daily routine

#### Shape and Space

**Conceptual understandings:**
- Shapes can be described and organized according to their properties
- Objects in our environment have a position that can be described

**Skills:**
- Understand 2D and 3D shapes have characteristics that can be compared
- Use common language to describe position: inside, outside, above, below, next to, behind, in front of, up, down
- Sort and compare 3D shapes
- Explore and describe paths, regions, and boundaries of immediate environment

**ISoP KG1-specific outcomes:** Sort, describe and name familiar 2D shapes (circle, square, rectangle, triangle, diamond, heart, oval); create patterns using familiar 2D shapes; use everyday language to describe position and direction (left, right, behind, next to); explore and describe paths, regions and boundaries

#### Data Handling

**Conceptual understandings:**
- We collect information to make sense of the world
- Organizing objects and events helps us solve problems
- Events in daily life involve chance

**Skills:**
- Understand sets can be organized by different attributes
- Understand information about surroundings can be obtained in different ways
- Discuss chance in daily events (impossible, maybe, certain)
- Represent information through pictographs and tally marks
- Sort and label real objects by attributes
- Create living graphs using real objects and people

### 2B. KG1 — English Literacy (Phase 1)

Sources: [IBO Language Scope and Sequence via IC Beirut](https://www.ic.edu.lb/uploaded/programs/IB_PYP_Program/PYP_language_scope_and_sequence.pdf); [Tema International School Benchmarks](https://www.tis.edu.gh/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/Language-Scope-Sequence-and-Benchmarks.pdf)

#### Oral Language — Listening and Speaking

**Skills:**
- Use gestures, actions, body language and/or words to communicate needs and express ideas
- Listen and respond to picture books showing pleasure and understanding
- Name classmates, teachers, and familiar classroom/playground objects
- Interact effectively with peers and adults in familiar social settings
- Tell own stories using words, gestures, and objects
- Repeat/echo single words; use single words and two-word phrases in context
- Join in with poems, rhymes, songs, and repeated phrases in shared books
- Understand simple questions and respond with actions or words
- Follow classroom directions and routines using context cues

**K1 Benchmarks (Tema International School):**
- Use body language and/or words to communicate
- Express ideas in a sentence
- Name familiar objects and people

#### Visual Language — Viewing and Presenting

**Skills:**
- Attend to visual information showing understanding through play, gestures, facial expression
- Recognize familiar signs, labels, and logos
- Make personal connections to visual texts
- Select and incorporate colours, shapes, symbols, and images into visual presentations
- Show appreciation of illustrations in picture books

#### Written Language — Reading

**Skills:**
- Enjoy listening to stories; choose and "read" picture books for pleasure
- Locate and respond to aspects of interest in self-selected texts
- Listen attentively and respond to stories read aloud
- Participate in shared reading, joining in with rhymes and repeated text
- Make connections to own experience when listening to or "reading" texts
- Begin to discriminate between symbols, numbers, letters, and words
- Recognize own first name
- Express opinions about meaning of a story; show empathy for characters
- Distinguish between pictures and written text
- Handle books showing understanding of how a book works (cover, beginning, directional movement, end)

#### Written Language — Writing

**Skills:**
- Experiment with writing using different implements and media
- Choose to write as play or in informal situations
- Differentiate between illustrations and written text
- Use own experience as stimulus when drawing and "writing"
- Participate in shared writing, observing teacher and making suggestions
- Begin to discriminate between letters, numbers, and symbols
- Show awareness of sound-symbol relationships; recognize how familiar sounds can be recorded
- Write own name independently
- Write an increasing number of frequently used words or ideas independently
- Illustrate own writing

### 2C. KG1 — Science/Inquiry (Age 3–5 Band, Early Part)

Sources: [Gjovik IB PYP Science Scope and Sequence](https://gjovikis.no/wp-content/uploads/2015/11/SCIENCE.pdf); [IBO Early Years Research Summary](https://www.ibo.org/globalassets/new-structure/research/pdfs/pypearlyyearsengweb.pdf)

**Overall expectation for 3–5 years:** Students develop observational skills using their senses to gather and record information, identify simple patterns, make predictions, and discuss ideas. They explore how objects and phenomena function, and recognize basic cause-and-effect relationships. They show care and respect for living things and the environment.

#### Eight Science Inquiry Skills (apply across all units)
1. Observe carefully in order to gather data
2. Use a variety of instruments and tools to measure data accurately
3. Use scientific vocabulary to explain observations and experiences
4. Identify or generate a question or problem to be explored
5. Plan and carry out systematic investigations
6. Make and test predictions
7. Interpret and evaluate data gathered in order to draw conclusions
8. Consider scientific models and applications (including their limitations)

**Note for KG1 (age 4.5):** Skills 4–8 are in emergent form. Focus is on 1 (careful observation), 3 (vocabulary), and 6 (simple predictions).

#### Science Content — KG1 (3–5 years age band)

**Living Things:**
- Observe and describe the characteristics of living and non-living things
- Observe the needs of living things that enable them to stay healthy
- Identify the parts of plants used by other living things (food, shelter)
- Be aware of the role of plants in sustaining life (providing oxygen, food)
- Show responsibility when caring for plants
- Take responsibility for living things found in the environment

**Earth and Space:**
- Talk about activities that occur during the day and night
- Compare activities that occur during the seasons
- Make connections between weather and how to protect oneself
- Identify simple patterns in daily and seasonal cycles
- Observe features of the local environment affected by daily/seasonal cycles

**Materials and Matter:**
- Use senses to describe observable properties of familiar materials (including solids, liquids, gases)
- Describe observable changes (including changes of state) in materials
- Recognize that materials can be solid, liquid, or gas
- Be aware of how to change water between states (solid, liquid, gas)
- Apply understanding of basic material properties to match materials to purpose (waterproofing, insulating)

### 3A. Grade 1 — Mathematics (Phase 2)

Sources: [IBO Maths Scope and Sequence via IC Beirut](https://www.ic.edu.lb/uploaded/programs/IB_PYP_Program/PYP_math_scope_and_sequence.pdf); [ISoP PYP Maths Scope and Sequence via Scribd](https://www.scribd.com/document/685049078/ISoP-PYP-Mathematics-Scope-and-Sequence)

#### Number

**Conceptual understandings:**
- The base 10 place value system is used to represent numbers and number relationships
- Fractions are ways of representing whole-part relationships
- Operations of addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division are related
- Number operations can be modelled in a variety of ways
- There are many mental methods for exact and approximate computations

**Knowledge and skills:**
| Category | Specific skills |
|----------|----------------|
| Construct meaning | Model numbers to hundreds using base 10 place value system; estimate quantities to 100 or beyond; model simple fraction relationships; use language of addition and subtraction (add, take away, plus, minus, sum, difference); model addition and subtraction of whole numbers; develop strategies for memorizing addition and subtraction facts; estimate sums and differences; understand situations involving multiplication and division |
| Transfer to symbols | Read and write whole numbers up to hundreds or beyond; read, write, compare and order cardinal and ordinal numbers; describe mental and written strategies for adding and subtracting two-digit numbers |
| Apply | Use whole numbers up to hundreds in real-life situations; use cardinal and ordinal numbers in real-life; use simple fraction names in real-life; use fast recall of addition and subtraction number facts; use mental and written strategies for two-digit addition/subtraction; select appropriate method (mental, written, or calculator); evaluate reasonableness of answers |

**ISoP Grade 1-specific outcomes:**
- Count to at least 100 by ones, twos, fives, and tens
- Read, write and represent numbers to at least 100
- Compare and order numbers using place value language (tens and ones)
- Add and subtract within 20 using mental strategies
- Identify and explain patterns in addition and subtraction
- Identify and model inverse relationship between addition and subtraction
- Introduction to fractions as equal parts of a whole (halves, quarters)

#### Pattern and Function

**Conceptual understandings:**
- Whole numbers exhibit patterns and relationships that can be observed and described
- Patterns can be represented using numbers and other symbols

**Skills:**
- Understand patterns in numbers (odd, even, skip counting)
- Understand inverse relationship between addition and subtraction
- Understand associative and commutative properties of addition
- Represent patterns using words, drawings, symbols, materials, actions, numbers
- Describe number patterns (odd/even, skip counting by 2s and 5s)
- Extend and create patterns
- Use number patterns to represent and understand real-life situations
- Use properties of addition and subtraction to solve problems

**ISoP Grade 1-specific outcomes:**
- Describe, extend and create number patterns (increasing and decreasing)
- Describe, extend and create number patterns formed by skip counting by twos

#### Measurement

**Conceptual understandings:**
- Standard units allow a common language to measure
- We use tools to measure attributes
- Estimation allows measurement with different levels of accuracy

**Skills:**
- Understand use of standard units (length, mass, money, time, temperature)
- Understand tools can be used to measure
- Understand calendars; identify and sequence days of the week and months
- Understand time using universal units (years, months, days, hours, minutes, seconds)
- Estimate and measure objects using standard units: length, mass, capacity, money, temperature
- Read and write time to the hour, half hour, and quarter hour
- Estimate and compare lengths of time: second, minute, hour, day, week, month
- Use measures of time to assist with problem solving

**ISoP Grade 1-specific outcomes:**
- Identify, measure and compare length, mass and capacity using non-standard units
- Use non-standard units to solve real-life measurement problems
- Identify, describe and sequence events in daily routine (after, before, tomorrow, today)
- Describe duration using months, weeks, days, hours
- Tell time to the full hour on analogue and digital clocks
- List days of the week, months of the year and seasons in order

#### Shape and Space

**Conceptual understandings:**
- Shapes are classified and named according to their properties
- Some shapes are made up of parts that repeat
- Specific vocabulary describes an object's position in space

**Skills:**
- Understand relationships among and between 2D and 3D shapes
- Understand 2D and 3D shapes can be created by combining/separating other shapes
- Understand examples of symmetry and transformations in the environment
- Sort, describe and label 2D and 3D shapes
- Analyse and describe relationships between 2D and 3D shapes
- Create and describe symmetrical and tessellating patterns
- Identify lines of reflective symmetry
- Represent ideas using geometric vocabulary (oral description, drawing, modelling, labelling)
- Interpret and create simple directions describing paths, regions, positions, boundaries

**ISoP Grade 1-specific outcomes:**
- Sort, describe and compare familiar 2D shapes (square, diamond, circle, triangle, rectangle, oval)
- Sort, describe and compare familiar 3D objects (ball shape, box shape) using features (corners, edges, faces)
- Create simple shape patterns
- Describe the position of an object (beside, in front of, up, down, next to, behind, between, below, above)

#### Data Handling

**Conceptual understandings:**
- Information can be expressed as organized and structured data
- Objects and events can be organized in different ways
- Some events in daily life are more likely to happen than others

**Skills:**
- Understand sets can be organized by one or more attributes
- Collect and record information in different ways
- Understand the concept of chance (impossible, less likely, maybe, most likely, certain)
- Collect and represent data in tally marks, bar graphs
- Represent relationships using tree, Venn, and Carroll diagrams
- Collect, display and interpret data for the purpose of answering questions
- Create pictographs and bar graphs; interpret data (more, fewer, less than, greater than)
- Identify and describe chance in daily events

### 3B. Grade 1 — English Literacy (Phase 2)

Sources: [IBO Language Scope and Sequence via IC Beirut](https://www.ic.edu.lb/uploaded/programs/IB_PYP_Program/PYP_language_scope_and_sequence.pdf); [Tema International School Benchmarks](https://www.tis.edu.gh/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/Language-Scope-Sequence-and-Benchmarks.pdf)

#### Oral Language — Listening and Speaking

**Skills:**
- Listen and respond in small or large groups for increasing periods of time
- Listen to and enjoy stories read aloud; show understanding through oral, written, or visual response
- Memorize and join in with poems, rhymes, and songs
- Follow classroom instructions, showing understanding
- Describe personal experiences
- Obtain simple information from accessible spoken texts
- **Distinguish beginning, medial, and ending sounds of words** with increasing accuracy ← phonemic awareness
- Follow two-step directions
- Predict likely outcomes when listening to texts read aloud
- Use language to address needs, express feelings and opinions, explore ideas
- Ask questions to gain information; respond to inquiries
- Use oral language during classroom activities, conversations, and imaginative play
- Talk about stories, writing, pictures, and models they have created
- Use grammatical rules of the instruction language (may over-generalize at this stage)
- Modulate volume and intonation appropriate to context

**Grade 1 Benchmarks:**
- Communicate orally in a variety of contexts
- Contribute relevant ideas to discussions
- Ask and answer simple questions
- Understand and follow two-step instructions
- Listen and respond to stories and discussions for increasing periods of time
- Sequence main events and ideas coherently in speech
- Speak at appropriate volume and pace
- Articulate sounds of words with increasing accuracy
- **Hear alphabet sounds in words (VC), (CV), and (CVC)** ← phonics milestone

#### Written Language — Reading

**Skills:**
- Select and reread favourite texts for enjoyment
- Understand that print is permanent
- Participate in shared reading, posing and responding to questions
- Participate in guided reading situations, applying reading behaviours
- Listen attentively and respond actively to read-aloud; make predictions, anticipate outcomes
- Read and understand meaning of self-selected and teacher-selected texts at appropriate level
- **Use meaning, visual, contextual, and memory cues; cross-check cues** ← cueing systems
- Read and understand familiar print from immediate environment (signs, logos, ICT iconography)
- Make connections between personal experience and storybook characters
- **Understand sound-symbol relationships and recognize familiar sounds/symbols/words** ← decoding
- **Instantly recognize an increasing bank of high-frequency and high-interest words** ← sight words
- Have secure knowledge of basic print conventions (orientation, directional movement, spacing, punctuation)
- Participate in reading aloud — taking roles, reading dialogue, reciting poems
- Discuss own experiences and relate to fiction and non-fiction texts
- Join in with chants, poems, songs, word games, gaining familiarity with sounds and patterns

#### Written Language — Writing

**Skills:**
- Enjoy writing and value own efforts
- Write informally about own ideas, experiences, feelings in personal journal using simple sentence structures ("I like…", "I can…", "I went to…")
- Read own writing to teacher and classmates
- Participate in shared and guided writing
- Write to communicate a message to a particular audience (news story, instructions, fantasy story)
- Create illustrations to match own written text
- Demonstrate awareness of conventions (sequence, spacing, directionality)
- **Connect written codes with sounds of spoken language** ← phonics in writing
- **Form letters conventionally and legibly** ← letter formation
- Discriminate between types of code (letters, numbers, symbols, words)

### 3C. Grade 1 — Science/Inquiry (Age 5–7 Band)

Source: [Gjovik IB PYP Science Scope and Sequence](https://gjovikis.no/wp-content/uploads/2015/11/SCIENCE.pdf)

**Overall expectation for 5–7 years:** Students develop observational skills using their senses to gather and record information, identify patterns, make predictions, and refine ideas. They explore how objects and phenomena function, identify parts of a system, and gain understanding of cause-and-effect relationships. They examine change over time and recognize that more than one variable may affect change.

**All eight inquiry skills apply (same as KG1 but with increasing sophistication):**
Skills 4–7 (question generation, investigation planning, variable manipulation, data interpretation) become more explicit and deliberate at Grade 1.

#### Science Content — Grade 1 (5–7 years age band)

**Living Things:**
- Recognize that living things, including humans, need resources for energy and growth
- Identify the major food groups and their role in human development
- Describe the life cycles of a variety of living things (animals and plants)
- Compare the life cycles of different living things
- Identify the common components of life cycles (birth, growth, maturity, reproduction, death)
- Investigate the responses of plants or animals to changes in their habitats

**Earth and Space:**
- Describe the natural features of local and other environments
- Analyse ways in which humans use the natural environment
- Identify or generate a question about human impact on the local environment

**Materials and Matter:**
- Reflect on and self-assess personal use of natural resources
- Investigate ways that familiar materials can be reused
- Group materials on the basis of properties for the purpose of recycling
- Describe how a particular material is recycled
- Explore the role of living things in recycling energy and matter

**Forces and Energy (emerges via Grade 1 unit themes):**
- Investigate and identify properties of air
- Examine how people use air in everyday lives (transportation, recreation)
- Reflect on the impact of air on living things
- Explore links between air, light, and sound (e.g., thunder and lightning)
- Recognize that imagination contributes to scientific developments
- Explore use of imagination as a tool to solve problems

## Section 4: Progression from KG1 to Grade 1

Sources: [IBO Maths Scope and Sequence via IC Beirut](https://www.ic.edu.lb/uploaded/programs/IB_PYP_Program/PYP_math_scope_and_sequence.pdf); [IBO Language Scope and Sequence via IC Beirut](https://www.ic.edu.lb/uploaded/programs/IB_PYP_Program/PYP_language_scope_and_sequence.pdf); [Gjovik Science Scope and Sequence](https://gjovikis.no/wp-content/uploads/2015/11/SCIENCE.pdf)

### Mathematics Progression

| Strand | KG1 (Phase 1) | Grade 1 (Phase 2) | Key Shift |

## Section 5: Common Misconceptions

Sources: [NAEYC — Mathematical Error in Kindergarten](https://www.naeyc.org/resources/pubs/yc/jul2017/mathematical-error-kindergarten); [Hechinger Report — Subitizing](https://hechingerreport.org/proof-points-subitizing/); [Education Endowment Foundation — Early Number](https://educationendowmentfoundation.org.uk/early-years/developing-childrens-early-understanding-of-numbers-two-key-research-findings-to-put-into-action); [Children's Science Misconceptions Survey](https://www.scribd.com/document/91663041/Children-Misconceptions); [Savvas — Phonics Misconceptions](https://www.savvas.com/resource-center/blogs-and-podcasts/savvas-insights/debunking-science-of-reading-myths-series/debunking-phonics-myths)

### Mathematics Misconceptions

| Misconception | Description | App Design Implication |
|--------------|-------------|----------------------|
| Rote counting ≠ number sense | Children recite "one, two, three..." without understanding cardinality — that the last word spoken names the quantity | Always require "how many?" after counting; use subitizing games rather than only counting |
| Subitizing errors | Showing dot patterns too long allows children to count; they then report the count not the perceived quantity, undermining the skill | Flash cards must display for < 2 seconds; never ask children to count the dots |
| Conservation of number failure | A child who correctly counts 6 objects, when those objects are rearranged in a longer line, will re-count and say "more" — they think spreading objects out increases quantity | Include transformation animations; ask "did the number change?" after rearranging |
| Linguistic number patterns misapplied | Children use language patterns incorrectly in math (e.g., "six times six is twenty-six" following a rhythm pattern) — they treat math as language, not quantity | Ensure number representations are always anchored to quantities, not just symbols |
| Place value: tens as "one ten" | Many Grade 1 children treat "23" as "2" and "3" not as 2 tens and 3 ones — place value is highly abstract | Use base-10 block visuals; bundle-of-tens animations; always show the physical grouping |
| Larger numeral = larger quantity (always) | When crossing decade boundaries (e.g., 9 vs. 10), some children think 9 is bigger because it "looks" older | Use number line animations; comparative exercises with concrete groupings |
| Addition/subtraction are separate | Children do not naturally see 3+4=7 implies 7-4=3; inverse relationship must be explicitly constructed | Design modules that show both sides of the equation; "undo" game mechanics |

### Literacy Misconceptions

| Misconception | Description | App Design Implication |
|--------------|-------------|----------------------|
| Letters and sounds are the same thing | Children believe a letter IS its sound, not that letters represent sounds | Always distinguish letter name from letter sound; use both consistently |
| Reading is memorization, not decoding | Some children at Phase 1 treat reading as memorizing the whole book; they "read" from memory not from print | Include print-tracking features; ensure text is not memorized but decoded |
| All words are phonetically regular | Children apply phonics rules to sight words (e.g., "the" pronounced "tuh-huh-eh") | Separate sight-word practice from phonics decode practice; use different visual cues |
| Initial sounds are the only sounds | Children can identify first sound but not medial or final sounds | Always practice in CVC order: initial → final → medial vowel (hardest) |
| Capital letters are different letters | Some early readers think "A" and "a" are different letters | Pair upper and lowercase consistently in all displays |
| Writing = copying | Children think writing means reproducing letters exactly, not encoding meaning | Celebrate invented spelling; show meaning-making before correctness |

### Science Misconceptions

| Misconception | Description | App Design Implication |
|--------------|-------------|----------------------|
| Plants are not living things (because they don't move) | Very common: children define "living" by self-movement only | Include plant growth time-lapses; growth and response to sunlight animations |
| Stones/rocks grow | Children see stones as natural objects that must therefore grow | Explicit comparison: living things need food/water/air and grow; stones do not |
| "Material" means cloth/fabric | Children use the everyday meaning of the word "material" | Always pair the word "material" with diverse examples: rock, plastic, wood, water |
| Heavy objects fall faster than light ones | Pre-Newtonian intuition from everyday experience | Use drop experiments; keep this for Grade 1 Forces exploration |
| Day and night are caused by the sun moving | Children observe the sun moving across the sky → conclude it moves around Earth | Use simple model animations showing Earth rotating, not the sun moving |
| Plants get their food from the soil | Children see gardeners adding soil and water → conclude soil IS food | Simplify: plants make food from sunlight + water (photosynthesis at concept level only) |
| Materials that look similar are the same material | Children say wood and plastic are the same because both feel similar | Include sensory property sorting: texture, hardness, whether it bends, floats vs. sinks |

## Section 7: Assessment Signals for the App

Sources: [IBO Assessment Report](https://ibo.org/contentassets/cc0cf81a37814d94b46b9711a50a1a98/final-report-ib-pyp-assessment.pdf); [PYP Teaching Tools — Assessment Strategies](https://www.pypteachingtools.com/blog/assessment-strategies-for-the-ib-pyp)

The IB PYP assessment philosophy emphasizes formative assessment, student agency, and learning-over-time evidence. Per the [IBO assessment standards](https://ibo.org/contentassets/cc0cf81a37814d94b46b9711a50a1a98/final-report-ib-pyp-assessment.pdf), key principles are:

1. Assessment should ultimately improve learning
2. Assessment should enable and motivate students to show what they can do
3. Assessment should combine self-assessment with other forms
4. Feedback is the most important component

### In-App Assessment Signals (Mapped to PYP Philosophy)

| Signal Type | What to Measure | How to Implement | PYP Alignment |
|-------------|----------------|------------------|---------------|
| **Accuracy over attempts** | % correct on first attempt vs. after hints | Track per-question accuracy by attempt number | Learning progression on continuum |
| **Consistency** | Same skill correct across 3+ sessions (not just once) | Only "master" a skill after 3 correct across different days | PYP: evidence of learning over time |
| **Speed/automaticity** | Time to respond to subitizing, sight words | Response time metrics; flag when child consistently needs > 4 seconds | Fluency vs. effortful processing |
| **Self-correction** | Does child self-correct before submitting? | Track edit events; reward self-correction as positive | PYP: metacognition, self-assessment |
| **Pattern of errors** | Which specific misconceptions recur? | Error-type tagging (e.g., "always guesses last shape in sequence") | Informs adaptive difficulty |
| **Engagement drop** | Module exits before completion; repeat skipping | Detect if same module is abandoned 3+ times | Flag for parent/redesign |
| **Transfer** | Applying skill in a novel context or theme | Every 5th session: same skill in different visual context | PYP: application with understanding |
| **Progression readiness** | Child consistently in top tier of Phase 1; ready for Phase 2 | Trigger Phase 2 modules when 80%+ accuracy across full Phase 1 strand | Phase-based progression |

### Parent-Facing Assessment Dashboard

Per the [IBO assessment report](https://ibo.org/contentassets/cc0cf81a37814d94b46b9711a50a1a98/final-report-ib-pyp-assessment.pdf), 81% of PYP teachers agreed that involving students in their own assessment improves learning outcomes. Recommended dashboard elements:

- **Skill map**: Visual continuum showing which Phase 1/Phase 2 outcomes are emerging, developing, or consolidated
- **Daily snapshot**: What was practiced today in plain language ("Anoushka practiced counting sets of objects up to 10")
- **Streak and consistency**: Days practiced in a row; total days practiced this week
- **Question to discuss**: One follow-up question to ask the child ("Can you find something at home that is heavier than something else?")
- **Misconception alert**: If a persistent error pattern is detected, surface it in plain language with a tip

## Section 8: Recommendations for Short Daily Sessions

Sources: [CNLD — Attention Span by Age](https://www.cnld.org/how-long-should-a-childs-attention-span-be/); [PMC — Attention Training Research](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12088968/); [PMC — Game-Based Learning Meta-Analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11018941/); [Funexpected Math Research](https://funexpectedapps.com/blog-posts/research-on-gamified-math-for-preschoolers)

### Optimal Session Parameters

| Parameter | KG1 (age 4.5) | Grade 1 (age 6.5) | Evidence |
|-----------|--------------|-------------------|---------|
| Attention span per task | 8–12 minutes total | 12–18 minutes total | [CNLD attention span data](https://www.cnld.org/how-long-should-a-childs-attention-span-be/) |
| Minimum effective session | 7–8 minutes | 10–15 minutes | [PMC attention training: 7–8 min sessions showed measurable gains](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12088968/) |
| Frequency | 3–4 times/week | 4–5 times/week | [Funexpected: even 2×15min/week accelerates learning](https://funexpectedapps.com/blog-posts/research-on-gamified-math-for-preschoolers) |
| Modules per session | 1–2 activities max | 2–3 activities max | Cognitive load; avoid fatigue |
| Session structure | Familiar warm-up → new challenge → reflection | Warm-up → challenge → extension/reflection | Matches PYP: constructing meaning → applying |
| Interleaving subjects | One subject per session for KG1 | Can interleave literacy + maths in one session | Working memory limitations at 4.5 |

### Session Design Principles

1. **Start with mastered skill** (first 2 minutes): Build confidence before introducing new content. PYP philosophy: learners need to feel agency.

2. **One new concept per session maximum**: Per working memory research and the PYP's emphasis on deep understanding over coverage.

3. **Break at natural stopping points**: Design each module to complete in under 5 minutes; sessions = multiple short modules, not one long activity.

4. **Parent or sibling involvement 1×/week**: The [IBO assessment report](https://ibo.org/contentassets/cc0cf81a37814d94b46b9711a50a1a98/final-report-ib-pyp-assessment.pdf) shows that family engagement significantly supports learning; build a weekly "play together" mode.

5. **Physical follow-through prompt**: Every session ends with a real-world action ("Go find 5 objects that are shorter than your finger"). Connects screen learning to the PYP's emphasis on action and real-world inquiry.

6. **No time pressure for KG1**: Age 4 is highly sensitive to performance anxiety. Timers should only appear as optional "challenge mode" for Grade 1.

7. **Choice and agency**: Offer 2–3 module choices at session start. PYP early years documentation [explicitly](https://www.copenhageninternational.school/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/The-Early-Learner-IB-Primary-Years-Programme.pdf) emphasizes "voice, choice, and ownership."

8. **Consistent session ritual**: Same intro animation, same closing celebration. Routine reduces transition anxiety for young learners.

### Subject Rotation Recommendation

```
Week example (KG1):
Mon: Maths (number/subitizing) — 8 min
Tue: Literacy (phonemic awareness) — 8 min
Wed: Maths (shape/pattern) — 8 min
Thu: Science (observation/living things) — 8 min
Fri: Free choice from completed modules — 8 min

Week example (Grade 1):
Mon: Maths (number + measurement) — 15 min
Tue: Literacy (phonics decode + sight words) — 15 min
Wed: Maths (pattern/data) — 12 min
Thu: Literacy (reading comprehension + writing) — 15 min
Fri: Science (inquiry module) — 12 min
```

## Section 9: Caveats About IB PYP Not Prescribing Fixed Content

This section consolidates the most important implementation-design caveats. Sources: [IBO Programmes page](https://www.ibo.org/programmes/primary-years-programme/curriculum/the-learner/); [Toddle 2025 Guide](https://www.toddleapp.com/learn/blog-post/2025-pyp-updates-your-guide-to-whats-changing-2/); [IBO Early Years Research](https://www.ibo.org/globalassets/new-structure/research/pdfs/pypearlyyearsengweb.pdf); [Structural Learning — Transdisciplinary Themes](https://www.structural-learning.com/post/6-ib-pyp-transdisciplinary-themes-guide)

### 1. Phases Are Not Grade Levels

The IB PYP's 2025 updated subject guides use four developmental phases that are "not age- or grade-bound." A child who is age 6.5 in Grade 1 might be in Phase 1 for writing and Phase 2 for number sense simultaneously. The app must be designed to allow skill-level selection independent of age or grade label.

### 2. Science Content Is School-Specific

The IB PYP does not mandate which science topics are taught at which year level. Schools develop their own Programme of Inquiry. Per [Structural Learning's analysis](https://www.structural-learning.com/post/6-ib-pyp-transdisciplinary-themes-guide), early years (ages 3–5) are required to complete only 4 units/year (vs. 6 for older years). A Dubai IB school's Grade 1 science may focus on habitats this year and forces next year. The app's science modules should be organized by *concept and inquiry skill*, not by a fixed content sequence.

### 3. Reading Instruction Is Not Specified by IB PYP

The IB PYP language scope and sequence describes *communication competencies*, not a phonics scope and sequence. Schools supplement with programmes like Letters and Sounds, Jolly Phonics, or Reading Workshop. The app should align with the competency descriptions (sound-symbol relationships, CVC blending, sight words, cueing strategies) without being prescriptive about phonics programme sequence.

### 4. Assessment Is Not Standardized by IB PYP Centrally

The IBO does not run standardized tests for PYP students. Schools choose their own external assessments (e.g., the [Scribd Grade 1 PYP guide](https://www.scribd.com/document/915407265/Grade-1-Curriculum-Guide) mentions DRA, GloSS, and JAM at one Dubai-region IB school). In-app assessments should not claim IB alignment; they should claim alignment with the skill continuums.

### 5. The Early Years Framework (Age 3–6) Explicitly De-Emphasizes Structured Skill Practice

The [IBO Early Learner document](https://www.copenhageninternational.school/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/The-Early-Learner-IB-Primary-Years-Programme.pdf) explicitly lists a move away from:
- "Literacy and numeracy experiences that develop skills through memorization and worksheets"

And toward:
- "Literacy and numeracy experiences that develop a wide range of **playful, inquiry-based explorations** into symbolic and representational learning"

For KG1 specifically, every module must feel like play, not drill. This is not cosmetic — it is the philosophical mandate of the programme the child is enrolled in.

### 6. The App Cannot Know Each Child's School's POI

Because each IB school writes its own Programme of Inquiry, the science and transdisciplinary units explored at the Dubai school are unknowable without direct access to that school's documentation. The app should position its science modules as *deepening and extending curiosity* about the four science strands, not as covering what the school is teaching this term.

### 7. April 2025 Subject Guides Are Behind IBO Login

The newest IB PYP subject guides (released April 17 and 22, 2025) are available only through the [IBO Programme Resource Centre](https://resources.ibo.org/pyp/) behind school login credentials. The publicly available pre-2025 scope and sequence documents (especially the Mathematics and Language continuums) remain the best accessible reference for the specific learning outcomes mapped in Sections 2–4 above. Schools using the 2025 guides may organize content slightly differently, but the core Phase 1 and Phase 2 competencies are preserved.

## Section 10: PRD-Ready Product Input for Claude Code

This section translates the curriculum research into a practical build brief. The product should treat IB PYP as a phase-based inquiry framework rather than a fixed grade checklist, because the PYP is built around learner agency, inquiry, and transdisciplinary learning rather than centrally mandated year-by-year content ([IB PYP curriculum framework](https://www.ibo.org/programmes/primary-years-programme/curriculum/)). The product should therefore model skills by learner phase and subject strand, not by a locked Grade 1 or KG1 syllabus.

### Problem Statement

Parents of young IB learners often want short, regular home practice that reinforces school learning without turning PYP into worksheets. The IB PYP emphasizes student agency, conceptual inquiry, play, and reflection ([IB Primary Years Programme](https://www.ibo.org/programmes/primary-years-programme/)), while public PYP mathematics and language continuums describe granular learning outcomes across phases rather than fixed grade checklists ([PYP Mathematics Scope and Sequence](https://www.ic.edu.lb/uploaded/programs/IB_PYP_Program/PYP_math_scope_and_sequence.pdf), [PYP Language Scope and Sequence](https://www.ic.edu.lb/uploaded/programs/IB_PYP_Program/PYP_language_scope_and_sequence.pdf)). For a 4.5-year-old KG1 learner and a 6.5-year-old Grade 1 learner, the app should deliver playful, high-frequency reinforcement in maths, English literacy, and science/inquiry while preserving developmentally appropriate practice.

### Product Goals

- **Daily reinforcement**: Enable each child to complete an age-appropriate session in under 10 minutes for KG1 and under 15 minutes for Grade 1, matching the short-session constraint and common age-related attention expectations described in the research file ([CNLD attention span guidance](https://www.cnld.org/how-long-should-a-childs-attention-span-be/)).
- **Phase-based mastery**: Track learning objectives by PYP Phase 1 and Phase 2 outcomes across mathematics, language, and science/inquiry rather than by rigid grade labels ([PYP Mathematics Scope and Sequence](https://www.ic.edu.lb/uploaded/programs/IB_PYP_Program/PYP_math_scope_and_sequence.pdf), [PYP Language Scope and Sequence](https://www.ic.edu.lb/uploaded/programs/IB_PYP_Program/PYP_language_scope_and_sequence.pdf)).
- **Playful practice**: Make KG1 practice feel like play and exploration, because IB early years materials emphasize inquiry-based learning through play for young children ([IB PYP Early Years brochure](https://ibo.org/globalassets/new-structure/interactive-development-toolkit/pdfs/pypeyheader-ib-brochure.pdf), [Inquiry through play](https://ibo.org/contentassets/117bf04eac9f45eda7d6b7afaf671ba0/inquiry-through-play-supporting-pyp-parents.pdf)).
- **Parent visibility**: Give parents a plain-language view of emerging, developing, and consolidated skills, with practical suggestions for at-home conversation and hands-on extension.
- **Implementation readiness**: Provide Claude Code with a structured objective library, module taxonomy, data model, acceptance criteria, and non-goals so the first version can be built without re-researching the curriculum.

### Non-Goals

- **No claim of official IB certification**: The app should not claim to be an official IB resource or to replicate any specific Dubai school’s Programme of Inquiry, because PYP schools design their own units within the framework ([IB PYP curriculum framework](https://www.ibo.org/programmes/primary-years-programme/curriculum/)).
- **No replacement for school instruction**: The app should be positioned as home reinforcement and enrichment, not as a full curriculum.
- **No long-form screen lessons for KG1**: KG1 modules should avoid lengthy explanations, worksheets, and extended drill, because early years PYP emphasizes play, agency, relationships, and inquiry ([IB PYP Early Years brochure](https://ibo.org/globalassets/new-structure/interactive-development-toolkit/pdfs/pypeyheader-ib-brochure.pdf)).
- **No broad multi-language scope in V1**: The current brief is English-only for literacy, so Hungarian literacy should remain outside V1.
- **No high-stakes assessment**: The app should use formative indicators, not exam-style grading, because PYP assessment is intended to support learning through feedback, reflection, and evidence over time ([IB PYP assessment report](https://ibo.org/contentassets/cc0cf81a37814d94b46b9711a50a1a98/final-report-ib-pyp-assessment.pdf)).

### Core Personas

- **KG1 learner, age 4.5**: Needs playful, visual, low-text interactions focused on Phase 1 number sense, oral language, emergent print concepts, observation, sorting, and simple cause-effect.
- **Grade 1 learner, age 6.5**: Needs slightly longer sessions with Phase 2 number operations, early decoding, sight words, reading comprehension, measurement, data, and deliberate science inquiry.
- **Parent**: Needs quick evidence of what was practiced, where the child struggled, and one simple off-screen extension prompt.
- **Future developer using Claude Code**: Needs structured objectives, module definitions, acceptance criteria, and data models that can be converted into app components.

### P0 Requirements

| Requirement | Description | Acceptance Criteria |
|---|---|---|
| Learner profiles | App supports two named child profiles with different phase defaults and session lengths. | Given the parent opens the app, when they select a child, then the app loads that child’s age band, default subjects, current skill states, and recommended session length. |
| Objective library | App stores objectives by subject, strand, phase, skill, misconception, and module type. | Given a developer adds a new objective, when it is saved, then it must include subject, phase, strand, objective text, success criteria, and at least one practice module type. |
| Short daily session engine | App generates a 1–3 module daily session based on child profile and mastery state. | Given KG1 is selected, when a session starts, then the session must target 1 subject and stay within the 8–10 minute design band. Given Grade 1 is selected, when a session starts, then it may interleave up to 2 subjects and stay within the 12–15 minute design band. |
| Maths V1 module set | App includes subitizing, counting/cardinality, pattern extension, shape sorting, and simple addition/part-whole modules. | Given the child completes a maths activity, when the result is saved, then the app records accuracy, attempts, response time where relevant, hint usage, and misconception tags. |
| Literacy V1 module set | App includes rhyme, initial sounds, letter matching, CVC decoding for Grade 1, sight words for Grade 1, and story sequencing. | Given a literacy module presents audio or visual prompts, when the child answers, then feedback must identify the target sound, word, or story relation without punitive language. |
| Science V1 module set | App includes living/non-living sorting, plant needs, material properties, day/night, observation notebook, and prediction prompts. | Given a science module runs, when the child completes it, then the app records an inquiry skill signal such as observe, classify, predict, explain, or reflect. |
| Adaptive progression | App adjusts difficulty from familiar success to one new challenge. | Given a child succeeds on a skill across 3 different sessions, when the next session is generated, then the app may raise difficulty or introduce a related transfer task. |
| Parent dashboard | App shows plain-language progress without grades. | Given the parent opens the dashboard, when data exists, then the app shows emerging/developing/consolidated skill states, recent misconceptions, and one recommended at-home prompt. |

### P1 Requirements

| Requirement | Description | Acceptance Criteria |
|---|---|---|
| Weekly rotation planner | Generates balanced weekly practice across maths, literacy, and science. | Given the parent chooses 4 sessions per week, when the week is planned, then the app creates a subject rotation that avoids repeating the same new skill on consecutive days. |
| Inquiry prompts | Adds off-screen activities after science and measurement sessions. | Given a session ends, when the objective has an off-screen extension, then the parent sees one prompt that can be done in under 5 minutes. |
| Misconception remediation | Routes repeated errors to targeted mini-games. | Given the same misconception appears twice in a week, when the next relevant session is generated, then the app includes a remedial module tagged to that misconception. |
| Voice/audio support | Reads prompts aloud for KG1 and early Grade 1. | Given the child has audio enabled, when an instruction appears, then a tap-to-hear button plays the instruction. |
| Session reflection | Child selects a simple feeling or confidence response at the end. | Given the session ends, when the reflection screen appears, then the child can choose “easy,” “just right,” or “tricky,” and the result is stored. |

### P2 Requirements

| Requirement | Description | Acceptance Criteria |
|---|---|---|
| Parent-created practice lists | Parent can pin specific objectives for extra practice. | Given the parent pins an objective, when sessions are generated, then that objective appears more often but not more than twice per week unless manually started. |
| Printable/off-screen cards | App generates simple non-screen activity cards. | Given a parent opens an objective, when they select “offline idea,” then the app displays a short activity using household objects. |
| Theme skins | Child can choose visual themes without changing learning logic. | Given a child selects a theme, when modules load, then the content and scoring remain unchanged while visuals update. |
| Teacher/tutor export | Parent can export a simple skill summary. | Given the parent selects export, when confirmed, then the app produces a shareable summary of practiced objectives and current skill states. |

### User Stories

- **As a KG1 learner**, I want to play a short counting or sound game so that I can practice without feeling like I am doing worksheets.
- **As a Grade 1 learner**, I want slightly harder challenges after I master an easier skill so that practice stays interesting.
- **As a parent**, I want to know which exact maths, reading, or science skill was practiced so that I can support it through conversation at home.
- **As a parent**, I want short sessions to stop automatically so that screen time stays controlled.
- **As a parent**, I want misconception alerts in plain language so that I understand whether my child is rote counting, confusing letter names with sounds, or guessing instead of observing.
- **As a developer**, I want each objective represented as structured data so that Claude Code can generate modules, routes, and progress logic from the same source of truth.

### Suggested Data Model

```ts
type LearnerPhase = "phase_1" | "phase_2";
type Subject = "maths" | "literacy" | "science";
type MasteryState = "not_started" | "emerging" | "developing" | "consolidated";

type LearningObjective = {
  id: string;
  subject: Subject;
  phase: LearnerPhase;
  strand: string;
  objective: string;
  childFriendlyGoal: string;
  prerequisiteObjectiveIds: string[];
  misconceptionTags: string[];
  moduleTypes: string[];
  successCriteria: string[];
  offlineExtension?: string;
};

type AttemptRecord = {
  learnerId: string;
  objectiveId: string;
  moduleId: string;
  startedAt: string;
  completedAt: string;
  correctFirstTry: boolean;
  attempts: number;
  responseTimeMs?: number;
  hintsUsed: number;
  errorTags: string[];
  reflection?: "easy" | "just_right" | "tricky";
};
```

### MVP Build Sequence

- **Sprint 1: Foundation**: Build learner profiles, objective schema, module shell, daily session engine, and local progress storage.
- **Sprint 2: Maths MVP**: Implement subitizing, counting/cardinality, pattern builder, base-10 builder, and part-whole machine.
- **Sprint 3: Literacy MVP**: Implement rhyme detective, letter twins, CVC sound builder, sight-word lightning, and sentence-picture matching.
- **Sprint 4: Science MVP**: Implement living/non-living sorter, material lab, day-night wheel, life-cycle puzzle, and predict-o-meter.
- **Sprint 5: Parent dashboard**: Add mastery states, misconception tags, session summaries, weekly rotation view, and one off-screen prompt per session.
- **Sprint 6: Polish and safety**: Add audio prompts, accessibility checks, screen-time stop states, reduced animation setting, and content QA.

### Success Metrics

| Metric | Target | Measurement |
|---|---|---|
| Completion | 80%+ of started sessions completed within target session length | Session start/end tracking |
| Retention | 3+ sessions per child per week after week 3 | Weekly active learner count |
| Mastery | 70%+ of practiced objectives move from emerging to developing/consolidated within 4 weeks | Objective state transitions |
| Parent usefulness | Parent opens dashboard at least once per week | Parent dashboard event |
| Misconception reduction | Repeated error tag frequency declines after remediation module | Error tag trend |
| Screen-time control | Less than 5% of sessions exceed intended time band | Session duration monitoring |

### Open Questions Before Coding

- **School POI alignment**: Should the app later allow the parent to enter the school’s current Unit of Inquiry so science modules can be prioritized?
- **Reading programme**: Does the school use a specific phonics sequence such as Jolly Phonics, Read Write Inc., or Letters and Sounds, and should the app mirror that sequence in a later version?
- **Content generation policy**: Should reading passages be authored manually in V1, AI-generated with review, or imported from licensed/parent-provided materials?
- **Storage and privacy**: Should child progress stay local-only in V1, or should it sync to a backend for multi-device use?
- **Voice and audio**: Should the app use browser text-to-speech initially, or pre-recorded human audio for higher quality?
